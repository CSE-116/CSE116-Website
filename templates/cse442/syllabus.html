Course Description

The value of this course largely depends on your choices. Make the most of it.

The intent of this course is to help bridge the gap between academia and industry by giving you the experience of working a as team of software engineers while you develop software. The course is open-ended as you are free to choose your own team and project.

Expectations

To do well in this course you are expected to:

  • Already be comfortable with all of the material covered in CSE115, CSE116, CSE250 an be ready to apply that material in a team setting. "I'm not a strong programmer" is not an excuse for not contributing to your project (Yes, students have tried using this as an excuse for higher grades that they did not earn)
  • Self-teach new material that you'll need for your project. This is a skill you will need throughout your career
  • Participate in lecture and your TA meetings every week and be prepared for each lecture/meeting
  • Effectively collaborate with your team, complete your assigned tasks, and participate in all meetings planed by your team
  • Comply with all the procedures of the project (user stories, testing, etc). It is unacceptable to just complete the code for your project
  • Complete at least 8 hours of work each week outside of lectures/TA meetings for a total of at least 12 hours/week on CSE442. The vast majority of this time will be spent on your project with 1-2 hours reserved for lecture prep
  • Convince the course staff that you have satisfied the requirements of the class (Only do this during grading demos)

You will be subjectively graded based on the amount of work you complete on your project compared to what a well prepared programmer (comfortable with CSE115, CSE116, CSE250) would complete in 8 hours/week. If your output is determined to be lower than that you will not receive full credit on your project.

Project Signup and Writeup

By the end of the second week of class you will submit your team and project idea as well as setup the structure (repository, scrum board) for the project. You must submit an appropriate project idea at this time. If the project is not appropriate you will be asked to re-submit a different idea and may lose your points for this submission.

Project ideas must only meet one criteria:

  • Once completed and used, the project must make a meaningful impact on the user's life

To meet this criteria you will write a description of your target user and explain how their life will be impacted. This impact does not have to significant, but it must be meaningful and direct. If you have to put a lot of thought into justifying this impact then it will not meet this criteria.

Note that this does imply a novelty component for your project since your software cannot have an impact on someone if there already exists software that solves the same problem. If your claim is that you have a better solution, then you must convince the course staff that your project can be completed by the end of the semester (ex. Making a better Hub from the ground up would have a meaningful impact, but is not feasible in a semester). If a working solution does not already exists you do not have to choose something that can be completed by the end of the semester. Dream big. You will be graded on your progress, not the finished product (though your software does have to run even if it doesn't meet it's ultimate goal).

Examples of software that would be denied:

  • A game (no impact, at least not in 1 semester), with exceptions such as educational games and games for users with disabilities
  • "Hello World" (No impact)
  • An app that helps you find recipes (Can't compete with existing apps in 1 semester)
  • A parking or car sharing app (Can't compete with existing apps in 1 semester)
  • Fantasy sports team builder/analyzer (Impact is not meaningful without significant justification)

For an idea to be accepted, the impact should be obvious. If your justification for impact sounds like it was written by a layer then it is not meaningful. For example, "Our investment app has impact because studies have shown that people with higher ROI are generally more happy. Our app increases ROI by helping users identify valuable trades..." In this case the impact is indirect and is caused by an increase in money. For this idea to be somewhat acceptable you would have to give your users enough free money to impact their lives.

The best way to come up with an idea is to start with a real problem that you care about and would like to help solve. There is no shortage of problems in the world and many have clear software solutions just waiting for a team of developers to implement. If your having trouble finding an idea that hasn't been solved already try focusing on a smaller area. Many cities have open data APIs. Try finding and API from your home town and working with their data.

The only exception to this criteria are sponsored project. There are a few projects affiliated with outside organizations which will be accepted even if their impact is questionable.

As with most grading in this course, this criteria is subjective and final approval will be made by the course staff.

Team Meetings

Starting week 3 your team will meet with a TA each week. During this meeting each team member will show their progress since the last meeting. During these meetings you must:

  • be on time
  • convince the TA that you've completed at least 8 hours of work since the previous meeting

Sprints

A detailed rubric used to grade your work will be made available for each sprint. The grading for each sprint will be accomplished by having your team give an end-of-sprint presentation (including a demo of the current release) to 2 members of the course staff. During this presentation, it is the team’s responsibility to demonstrate that they met each of the criteria from the grading rubric. You will also need to answer questions the grading staff have. During this presentation you be graded based on three primary categories: The work completed on the project and your ability to deploy it on a new machine, the documentation and organization of the project (including user stories, task tracking, git branching, commit messages, and scrum board), and project testing (unit tests, testing scripts, and acceptance tests).

Multiplying the number of team members by the team’s score on the presentation calculates the total number of individual points available for that sprint. Everyone will also complete a peer- and self-evaluation on how well each person contributed to the team during the sprint. Each student’s score is calculated by multiplying the total number of team points available to the average percentage of points they received on those evaluations. This means your grade depends not only on excellence in software engineering, but also on being a good teammate.

The TA with whom you meet each week will NOT have any input into the team’s sprint grade. This ensures open and honest communication during those meetings AND reflects the separation of development and testing found in larger organizations.

Details about how to sign up for these end-of-sprint presentations will occur closer to each of the deadlines.

Final Presentation

Your team will present your project in a style and with content that is appropriate for a commercial trade show. These presentations will occur in the final two weeks of the course and during the final exam period. All students are required to attend all of these presentations. A detailed grading rubric for these presentations will be available on the course website several weeks before the presentations begin. Teams will also be able to sign up for the time they wish to present at that time. These sign-ups will be done on a first-come, first-served basis.

Lecture Activities

Throughout the semester, teams will work on an in-class activities related to the course material. Each of these activities will have a deliverable or task to be completed before the end of lecture.

Student submissions will need to list the names of all of the group members who participated in creating the response. Listing a student who did not attend or attended but did not contribute is an academic integrity policy violation by both the student whose account is being used and the student answering the questions.

Lecture Discussions

In addition to lecture activities there will be lecture discussions throughout the semester. These activities will require that students reflect on software engineering concepts before writing a few paragraphs on the way they think is best to bring those concepts into practice. The documents students write will be submitted via AutoLab and scored holistically. Since these activities will also be used to assess department-level student learning outcomes, it is important that all students participate.

When discussing more divided topics the discussion will be structures asa debate allowing us to see both sides of the debate and gain an understanding for points of view that differ from our own.

Course Academic Integrity Policy: Do not represent work as your own if you did not create it.

All violations will be result in:

An F in CSE442.

It is your responsibility to understand what constitutes an academic integrity violation. If you have any question whether something you are doing is a violation or not, ask for clarification before receiving an F in the course. I will not entertain excuses after you have been caught.

Examples of acceptable behavior:

  • Searching the Internet for coding help and examples
  • Using libraries and frameworks in your project
  • Expanding an existing project as your course project
Examples of unacceptable behavior:
  • Copying a large amount of code into your project without crediting the source.
  • Allowing someone outside your group to write code for your project and representing it as the group's work.
  • Violating the licensing agreement of publicly available code.
  • Attempting to earn credit for a lecture activity without attending that lecture.
  • Breaking laws.
